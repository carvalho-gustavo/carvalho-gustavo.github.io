The expansion of renewable energy is reshaping power markets around the world. In Brazil, this transformation is particularly relevant because the electricity sector has historically relied on long-term forward contracts as a core mechanism to support investment, manage revenue stability, and ensure supply adequacy.

However, as wind and solar generation increase their participation in the power matrix, traditional contracting structures become less aligned with the physical and economic reality of renewable production. Most long-term contracts are still negotiated as flat energy products, while renewable generation is intrinsically variable across hours, days, seasons, and regions.

This creates a fundamental risk for renewable generators: the mismatch between contracted energy and actual hourly generation. When a generator is short relative to its contracted position, it must settle the difference at spot prices, which may be high precisely when generation is low. This is known as **price-and-quantity risk**.

## The hidden risk in renewable PPAs

Long-term Power Purchase Agreements, or PPAs, have played a critical role in the development of the Brazilian power sector. They provide revenue visibility for investors and reduce dependence on short-term spot price signals.

But for variable renewable energy sources, a flat forward position may create exposure. A solar plant, for example, produces mostly during daylight hours. A wind plant may produce more intensely during specific periods of the day or night, depending on regional wind patterns. A flat contract does not reflect this hourly production profile.

The result is that renewable generators may be overcontracted in some hours and undercontracted in others. This exposure becomes more material when spot prices are volatile or negatively correlated with generation.

In other words, the issue is not only how much energy a renewable plant produces on average. The issue is **when** that energy is produced and **what the spot price is at that time**.

## Why hourly granularity matters

Hourly granularity matters because value and risk in power markets are increasingly time-dependent.

As renewable penetration increases, the average price level becomes less informative than the hourly price profile. A generator that is well hedged on an annual or monthly basis may still be exposed on an hourly basis.

This is especially important in markets where forward contracting remains the dominant commercial mechanism, but the underlying generation mix is becoming more intermittent and weather-driven.

A more granular forward market can help bridge this gap. Instead of relying only on flat contracts, market participants could use hourly forward products to reshape their exposure across the day.

The central idea is simple: renewable generators should be able to adjust their forward position according to their expected hourly generation profile and risk appetite.

## Wind-solar complementarity in Brazil

The Brazilian system offers an important source of value: the complementarity between wind and solar generation.

Solar generation is concentrated during daylight hours. Wind generation, depending on the region, may present a different hourly profile. In the Northeast of Brazil, this complementarity can be particularly relevant.

This means that different renewable agents may have opposite hedging needs across the day. A solar generator may want to buy protection during hours without sunlight and sell protection during the day. A wind generator may have the opposite incentive in certain periods.

This complementarity creates the foundation for a market-based hedging mechanism. Instead of requiring common ownership of assets, centralized pooling, or complex bilateral arrangements, agents could trade standardized financial contracts linked to specific delivery hours.

## The proposed mechanism: hourly forward contracts

The paper proposes a decentralized market for hourly forward contracts, structured as financial instruments for specific hours of delivery.

In practical terms, the mechanism considers 24 simultaneous products, one for each hour of the day. Each product represents a forward contract associated with a specific hourly block over a week-ahead horizon.

Through these instruments, renewable generators can buy or sell hourly exposure and reshape their total forward position. A generator with surplus expected production in a given hour may sell an hourly contract. A generator with expected deficit in another hour may buy one.

The mechanism does not require physical delivery between agents. It is a financial hedging structure settled against spot prices.

This is important because it preserves operational simplicity while allowing market participants to adjust their commercial exposure. It also avoids some of the practical barriers of alternative approaches, such as asset acquisition, centralized renewable pools, or the need to share detailed generation information among participants.

## What the numerical experiment shows

The study evaluates the proposed mechanism using real data from the Brazilian power system.

The numerical experiment considers 28 renewable plants connected to the Brazilian grid: 13 wind power plants and 15 solar power plants. The analysis compares two cases:

1. A base case without the hourly hedging market.
2. A market case in which renewable agents can trade hourly forward contracts.

The results indicate a meaningful improvement in market welfare. For the representative set of renewable agents, the proposed mechanism increases overall welfare by **9.5%**.

The benefits are also visible at the individual-agent level. The certainty-equivalent value increases for all agents in the experiment. On average, the increase is approximately **30%**, with around **20% for wind plants** and **36% for solar plants**.

These results suggest that the mechanism is not merely reallocating value from one group to another. It is improving risk-adjusted outcomes by allowing agents with complementary profiles to exchange exposure more efficiently.

## The economic intuition

The intuition behind the result is straightforward.

When solar generation is high during the day, solar agents can sell hourly contracts and monetize their relatively stronger position. During nighttime hours, when solar production is absent, they may buy contracts to reduce their exposure.

Wind generators may take the opposite side of these trades depending on their hourly generation profile and risk exposure. The result is a financial exchange of surplus and deficit positions across hours.

This allows agents to better align their contracted position with their expected generation profile. As a consequence, the exposure to unfavorable spot market settlement is reduced.

The paper also finds evidence of a positive risk premium in hourly hedging prices. This reflects the value that risk-averse renewable agents assign to reducing exposure to price-and-quantity risk.

## Strategic implications for Brazil’s power market

The implications for Brazil are significant.

First, the development of hourly forward products could improve risk management for renewable generators. As the share of intermittent generation increases, this becomes increasingly important.

Second, more granular hedging instruments could support greater confidence in long-term contracting. The study indicates that the availability of hourly hedging could allow renewable agents to increase forward involvement while maintaining or improving risk-adjusted welfare.

Third, the mechanism could contribute to the evolution of Brazil’s power market design. The Brazilian market has historically relied on long-term forward contracts, but the increasing relevance of intraday variability suggests that more granular products will become necessary.

Finally, hourly forward contracts could help transform renewable complementarity into market value. Instead of treating variability only as a system operation challenge, the market could create instruments that allow participants to manage and price this variability.

## Conclusion

The energy transition requires more than new generation assets. It also requires new market instruments.

As renewable penetration increases, power markets need products that reflect the temporal profile of generation, consumption, and price formation. Flat forward contracts will remain important, but they are not sufficient to address all risks created by variable renewable energy.

Hourly forward contracts offer a promising path. They allow renewable agents to reshape their exposure, manage price-and-quantity risk, and extract value from the complementarity between wind and solar generation.

For Brazil, this type of instrument could represent an important step in the modernization of the forward market, supporting more robust renewable integration and more sophisticated risk management practices.

The broader lesson is clear: as power systems become more renewable, market design must become more granular.
